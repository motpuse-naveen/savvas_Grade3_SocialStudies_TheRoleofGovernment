In This Text Set

This text set includes four informational texts and a video. All deal with an aspect of how the American government works.

Why Do We Have a Government? is the anchor text for this set. Depending on the needs of your class, you may choose to assign Why Do We Have a Government? for independent reading, or you may choose to read it aloud with the class. Beginning with the anchor text will ground students in concepts and academic vocabulary that are key to discussing the Big Question. As students read the other texts in the set, they will compare their new understandings, as well as the structure and features of the text, with the anchor text.

After students read the anchor text, you may choose to assign specific texts or encourage students to choose the texts that appeal to them the most. Use the table below to see where specific text structures and text features appear across the texts in this set.

Standards and Objectives

  • Social Studies Standard

    Explain the origins, functions, and structure of different systems of government, including those created by the U.S. and state constitutions.

  • Informational Text Focus

    In this text set, students will read informational texts to understand how descriptive, chronological, and compare and contrast text structures and text features such as section heads and photographs help their understanding as they read.

  • Content Objective

    Students will understand how the United States government works, including the roles and responsibilities of each level and branch of the government.

  • Language Objective

    Students will use what they have learned about government to write a letter to a government representative.

Why Do We Have a Government?

by Maria Nicols
Informational Text - Word Count: 504   Lexile: 760L

Instructional Focus

  • Social Studies Standard Explain the origins, functions, and structure of different systems of government, including those created by the U.S. and state constitutions.

  • Content Objective Students will understand the purpose and responsibilities of governments.

  • Language Objective Students will describe the purpose of government and how government affects people’s lives.

  • Text Structure Objective Students will understand how a descriptive text structure explains or defines an idea, using examples.

  • Text Feature Objective Students will understand how headings help a reader understand the different parts of a text and what each section will explain.

Summary

This informational text explains how governments can help people by providing services to citizens.

Before Reading

Big Question

Remind students of the Big Question: What role does the government play in improving our lives? Say: Why do you think we have a government? In what ways could the government help people or improve their lives?

Preview Vocabulary

Have students find and read sentences in the text that have the words government, responsibility, and service. Discuss the meaning of each word with the class. Say: The government includes people who make laws and people who lead a city, town, or country. Who are some people in the government? What is one of your responsibilities at school? A service is a way of giving someone help. The government gives services, such as helping kids go to school. What is another example of a service?

Oral Vocabulary

Some of the vocabulary in Why Do We Have a Government? includes words that might be new or challenging. During class discussions, use these new terms repeatedly to help students become familiar with them. For example: What makes a country a democracy? Who can vote in a democracy? A legislature can make laws. What kinds of laws do you think our state’s legislature should pass?

Word Webs

Tell students that one way to learn new words is to think about how a new word is connected to other words they already know. Display a word web graphic organizer with government in the center. Have students copy their own word webs in their notebooks. Ask them to add to the web with words they already know as well as new vocabulary words for Why Do We Have a Government? A completed word web might look like this:

[insert word web with government in the center and these words branching around it:
president service legislature democracy]

During Reading

Elements Of Informational Text

Text Structure Say: This is a descriptive text. It helps define or explain something, using examples. Look at the title. What do you think the text is describing? (government) Read screens 2 and 3 (Printable Text p. x). Ask: What is one responsibility of the government? (keeping people safe) Ask students to look for more details and examples about government responsibilities as they read.

Display a T-chart graphic organizer. At the top of the left column, write “Descriptive Structure.” Work with the class to fill in the column with what they know about descriptive structure and how it helps them understand the writer’s ideas. For example: “tells about what something is or what it is like,” “gives details or examples”

Text Features Explain that headings help people understand what they read by introducing the topic of a section. Say: This text has different sections, or parts, with headings. What is the first heading you see? (What Is a Government?) What do you think that section will be about? Have students preview other headings and predict what they will read about in each section.

Return to the T-chart. At the top of the right column, write “Headings.” Work with the class to fill in the column with examples of how headings help readers understand the text. For example: “tell the reader what a section is about”

After Reading

Vocabulary

After students have read Why Do We Have a Government?, have them review their word webs. Ask: Are there any words you’d like to add? Display these images from Why Do We Have a Government? to help students think about words such as service, democracy, and legislature.

[images from the book for each word for the teacher to project: TK]

Discuss Across Texts

Encourage students to make connections between Why Do We Have a Government? and other texts they have read on related subjects.

  • What else have you read about the government? What did you know about the government before reading this text?
  • This text describes government workers who help their communities, such as firefighters and police officers. What else have you read about people with these jobs?
  • In Why Do We Have a Government? you learned that the government helps run libraries, schools, and parks. What else have you read about these places?

Track the Texts: After the discussion, have students return to the The Role of Government: Track the Texts graphic organizer. Have them fill in the Why Do We Have a Government? row with something they learned about how the government works.

Discuss The Big Question

Lead a class discussion around this text’s Big Question: What role does the government play in improving our lives? As students share their ideas, draw out more information from them with follow-up questions such as, “Can you say more about that?” or “What are some examples of that?”

  • What are some examples of people who work for the government? What do they do to help other people?
  • Why do you think we need both a local government and a national government? What role does each part of the government play in our lives?

Write About The Big Question

Remind students of some of the government jobs they read about in the text. Ask them to write a paragraph about which government job they would choose and how people in that job can help people.

Levels of Government

adapted from myWorld
Informational Text - Word Count: 646   Lexile: 810L

Instructional Focus

  • Social Studies Standard Explain the origins, functions, and structure of different systems of government, including those created by the U.S. and state constitutions.

  • Content Objective Students will understand the responsibilities and roles of different levels of government, including local, state, and national.

  • Language Objective Students will explain how different levels of government affect their lives.

  • Text Structure Objective Students will understand how a compare and contrast text structure describes similarities and differences between things.

  • Text Feature Objective Students will understand how photos help readers understand the information in a text.

Summary

This informational text explains the roles and responsibilities of three different levels of government (federal, state, and local).

Before Reading

Big Question

Remind students of the Big Question: What role does the government play in improving our lives? Say: The different levels of our government include city or town governments, the state government, and the national government. How do you think each level of government can work to improve people’s lives?

Preview Vocabulary

Have students find and read sentences in the text that have the words federal, local, and elect. Discuss the meaning of each word with the class. Say: People elect leaders by voting for them. What is one elected leader you know about? The federal government is the government of the whole country. Who is a member of the federal government? How would you describe your local area, or the area where you live?

Oral Vocabulary

Some of the vocabulary in Levels of Government includes words that might be new or challenging, such as legislature and service. During class discussions, use these new terms repeatedly to help students become familiar with them. For example: What does a legislature do? What are some services that the government provides?

Word Webs

Tell students that one way to learn new words is to think about how a new word is connected to other words they already know. Display a word web graphic organizer. Help students practice with a word web about levels of government. When they read Levels of Government, encourage them to add words to their web that relate to the word government.

During Reading

Elements Of Informational Text

Text Structure Say: This text has a compare and contrast structure. It shows how two or more things are similar and different. As you read, look for words such as same, like, different, and but. These are clue words that show the writer is comparing or contrasting. How do you think local and national governments are similar? (Sample response: They are both supposed to help people.) How might they be different? (Sample response: They are in charge of different things.)

Display a T-chart graphic organizer. At the top of the left column, write “Compare and Contrast Structure.” Work with the class to fill in the column with what they know about compare and contrast structure and how it helps them understand the writer’s ideas. For example: “tells about how things are the same and different”

Text Features Preview the photos on screen 3 (Printable Text p. x). Say: These photos show people who work in a local government. A local government is usually the government of a city or town. Ask students to describe what they see. Ask: How do these photos help you understand what a local government does?

Return to the T-chart. At the top of the right column, write “Photographs.” Work with the class to fill in the column with examples of how photos help readers understand the text. For example: “help readers understand ideas from the text”

After Reading

Vocabulary

After students have read Levels of Government, have them review their word webs. Ask: Are there any words you’d like to add?

Display these images from Levels of Government to help students as they think about vocabulary words, such as federal, local, and elect.

[images from the book for each word for the teacher to project: TK]

Discuss Across Texts

Encourage students to make connections between Levels of Government and other texts they have read on related subjects.

  • This text explains what people do in some government jobs. What else have you read about people who work for the government?
  • What did you read about the responsibilities of the government? What else have you read about government responsibilities?
  • Think back to Why Do We Have a Government? You read about how governments can help people. In Levels of Government, what else did you learn about how government can help?

Track the Texts: After the discussion, have students return to the The Role of Government: Track the Texts graphic organizer. Have them fill in the Levels of Government row with something they learned about how government works.

Discuss The Big Question

Lead a class discussion around this text’s Big Question: What role does the government play in improving our lives? As students share their ideas, draw out more information from them with follow-up questions such as, “Can you say more about that?” or “What are some examples of that?”

  • What is one responsibility of the local government? What about the national government?
  • Why is it important to have different levels of government? How can each level of government improve people’s lives?

Write About The Big Question

Help students identify who represents them at different levels of government, such as their mayor and/or city council member, their state representative and governor, their congressperson and senator, the president and vice president. Have them write a paragraph about how these government representatives can help people.

Branches of Government

adapted from myWorld
Informational Text - Word Count: 612   Lexile: 780L

Instructional Focus

  • Social Studies Standard Explain the origins, functions, and structure of different systems of government, including those created by the U.S. and state constitutions.

  • Content Objective Students will understand how the three branches of government divide the responsibilities of government.

  • Language Objective Students will explain the roles and responsibilities of the three branches of government.

  • Text Structure Objective Students will understand how a compare and contrast text structure identifies similarities and differences between things.

  • Text Feature Objective Students will understand how photographs support a reader’s understanding of the information in the text.

Summary

This informational text explains the roles and responsibilities of the three branches of the United States government.

Before Reading

Big Question

Remind students of the Big Question: What role does the government play in improving our lives? Say: There are three branches of government: executive, legislative, and judicial. These branches make laws, enforce laws, and decide what the laws mean. How do you think each branch could improve people’s lives?

Preview Vocabulary

Have students find and read sentences in the text that have the words executive, legislative, and judicial. Discuss the meaning of each word with the class. Say: The word legislative comes from the word for laws. What do you think the legislative branch does? Do judges work in the judicial branch? The president is the head of the executive branch. What do you think some of the president’s main responsibilities are?

Oral Vocabulary

Some of the vocabulary in Branches of Government includes words that might be new or challenging. During class discussions, use these new terms repeatedly to help students become familiar with them. For example: A branch of government can check, or limit, another branch’s powers. How can the president check the power of Congress? How does the Supreme Court check the power of the president?

Word Webs

Tell students that one way to learn new words is to think about how a new word is connected to other words they already know. Display a word web graphic organizer. Help students practice by drawing a word web about immigration or transportation. When they read Branches of Government encourage them to add words to their web that relate to the word branches.

[Create a word web with central word branches. Use a central circle and small circles around the circle, connected with lines. Around the outside of the central circle, add these words:]
legislative judicial executive

During Reading

Elements of Informational Text

Text Structure Say: This is a compare and contrast text. When writers compare, they explain how things are similar, or the same. When they contrast, they explain how things are different. Point out the word different on screen 2 (Printable Text p. x). Ask: What is the writer contrasting? (the branches of government)

Display a T-chart graphic organizer. At the top of the left column, write “Compare and Contrast Structure.” Work with the class to fill in the column with what they know about compare and contrast structure and how it helps them understand the writer’s ideas. For example: “tells about how things are similar and different”

Text Features Point out the three photographs on screen 3 (Printable Text p. x). Say: What do you see in the photographs? Read the text associated with each photo. Ask students how the photos help them understand the text. Guide them to understand that the photos show where people in each branch of government work.

Return to the T-chart. At the top of the right column, write “Photographs.” Work with the class to fill in the column with examples of how photographs help readers understand the text. For example: “give more details about information in the text”

After Reading

Vocabulary

After students have read Branches of Government, have them add any words to their word web. Display these images from Branches of Government to help students as they think about vocabulary words, such as legislative, executive, and judicial.

[images from the book for each word for the teacher to project: TK]

Discuss Across Texts

Encourage students to make connections between Branches of Government and other texts they have read on related subjects.

  • There are three branches of government: executive, legislative, and judicial. What have you read about the branches of government in other texts?
  • This text describes different responsibilities of the government. What else have you read about the government’s responsibilities?
  • In Why Do We Have a Government? you read that the government helps keep people safe. How does each branch of government help protect people?

Track the Texts: After the discussion, have students return to the The Role of Government: Track the Texts graphic organizer. Have them fill in the Branches of Government row with something they learned about how government works.

Discuss The Big Question

Lead a class discussion around this text’s Big Question: What role does the government play in improving our lives? As students share their ideas, draw out more information from them with follow-up questions such as, “Can you say more about that?” or “What are some examples of that?”

  • What are checks and balances? Why are checks and balances important?
  • How do the checks and balances make sure that no part of the government has unlimited power?
  • How would you describe the system of checks and balances? How do you think checks and balances benefit everyday people?

Write About the Big Question

Have students create a drawing to illustrate how parts of the government work together and write a paragraph explaining their drawing.

Capitol Visitor Center: At the Nation’s Legislature

Video -4 minutes

Instructional Focus

  • Social Studies Standard Explain the origins, functions, and structure of different systems of government, including those created by the U.S. and state constitutions.

  • Content Objective Students will understand what the US Capitol is like, who works there, and how laws are made.

  • Language Objective Students will describe a fictional visit to the US Capitol or another governmental building, along with an imagined encounter with a political figure.

  • Text Structure Objective Students will understand how a video’s descriptive structure shows details about things and ideas.

  • Text Feature Objective Students will understand how photographs can help viewers understand the main ideas or events in a video.

Summary

This video invites viewers to join a girl’s visit to the US Capitol, where she learns about the history and purpose of the US Congress.

Before Viewing

Big Question

Remind students of the Big Question: What role does the government play in improving our lives? Say: The US Congress makes up the legislative branch in our government. Congress meets at the Capitol. How do you think learning about the US Capitol will help you understand how parts of the government work together?

Preview Vocabulary

Discuss the meaning of the words legislative and bill. Say: The legislative branch of the government makes laws. Who do you think is part of the legislative branch? A bill is an idea for a law. How do you think a bill could become a law?

Oral Vocabulary

Some of the vocabulary in Capitol Visitor Center includes words that might be new or challenging. During class discussions, use these new terms repeatedly to help students become familiar with them. For example: Does our state have a large population? Which state do you think has the biggest population in the country?

Word Webs

Tell students that one way to learn new words is to think about how a new word is connected to other words they already know. Display a word web graphic organizer. Help students practice with a word web about the legislative branch of the government. When they watch the video, encourage them to add words to their web that relate to the word legislative. A completed word web might look like this:

[insert word web with “legislative” in the center and these words around the web:]
government representative bill law

During Viewing

Elements of Informational Text

Text Structure This video has a descriptive structure. Say: This video is descriptive. It describes a girl’s visit to the US Capitol. What do you think might be described in the video? (Responses may include “what the Capitol looks like” or “people she sees at the Capitol.”)

Display a T-chart graphic organizer. At the top of the left column, write “Descriptive Structure.” Work with the class to fill in the column with what they know about descriptive structure and how it helps them understand the writer’s ideas. For example: “tells what something is like”

Text Features Say: This video includes photographs of the Capitol. As you watch the video, think about how the photos help you understand what the Capitol looks like and what people do there. Pause the video at 1:15 and ask students to describe what they see. Ask: How do the pictures help you understand the history of the Capitol?

Return to the T-chart. At the top of the right column, write “Photographs.” Work with the class to fill in the column with examples of how photos help readers understand a video or text. For example: “help viewers understand what a building looks like and what people do there”

After Viewing

Vocabulary

After students have watched the video, ask them to review their word webs. Ask: Are there any words you’d like to add?

Discuss Across Texts

Encourage students to make connections between Capitol Visitor Center and texts they have read on related subjects.

  • What else have you read about the United States government and how it works?
  • The video showed museums that are located near the Capitol. Have you ever read about or visited museums like those? What did you read about them, what did you see?
  • Think back to Why Do We Need a Government? That text describes the reason for having a government. How did Capitol Visitor Center help you understand why we have a government?

Track the Texts: After the discussion, have students return to the The Role of Government: Track the Texts graphic organizer. Have them fill in the Capitol Visitor Center row with something they learned about how government works.

Discuss The Big Question

Lead a class discussion around this text’s Big Question: What role does the government play in improving our lives? As students share their ideas, draw out more information from them with follow-up questions such as, “Can you say more about that?” or “What are some examples of that?”

  • Who are some people who work in the Capitol? What role do they play in the government?
  • Think about Zana’s description of how a bill becomes a law. How can a law help improve people’s lives?

Write About the Big Question

Have students imagine taking their own tour of the Capitol or other government buildings in Washington, DC. Ask them to write a description of their tour, including what they see and what people do in each location. Have them explain how the people who work at the Capitol can help others.

How the Constitution Works

adapted from myWorld
Informational Text - Word Count: 549   Lexile: 760L

Instructional Focus

  • Social Studies Standard Explain the origins, functions, and structure of different systems of government, including those created by the U.S. and state constitutions.

  • Content Objective Students will understand why the Constitution is an important document in the United States and how it helped shape the US government.

  • Language Objective Students will explain the importance of the US Constitution.

  • Text Structure Objective Students will understand how a chronological text structure explains how events happened in sequence.

  • Text Feature Objective Students will understand how headings help a reader understand the different parts of a text and what each section will explain.

Summary

This informational text explains how the Constitution was written and what it does.

Before Reading

Big Question

Remind students of the Big Question: What role does the government play in improving our lives? Explain that the US Constitution sets out the main system of laws in the country. Say: The Constitution helps explain how the government works in the United States. Why do you think it’s important to have a constitution? How does the Constitution improve people’s lives?

Preview Vocabulary

Have students find and read sentences in the text that have the words responsibility and Constitution. Discuss the meaning of each word with the class. Say: The US government has different parts. What are some of the responsibilities of each part of the government? The Constitution is a piece of writing that explains how the government works in the United States. Why is having a constitution important?

Oral Vocabulary

Some of the vocabulary in How the Constitution Works includes words that might be new or challenging. During class discussions, use these new terms repeatedly to help students become familiar with them. For example: The purpose of checks and balances was to make sure no part of the government had too much power. What is one way a branch of the government can check, or limit, another branch?

Word Webs

Tell students that one way to learn new words is to think about how a new word is connected to other words they already know. Display a word web graphic organizer. Help students practice with a word web about the Constitution. When they read How the Constitution Works, encourage them to add words to their web that relate to this word. A completed word web might look like this:

[insert word web with constitution in the center, and these words around the web:] checks balances government amendment

During Reading

Elements of Informational Text

Text Structure Say: This is a chronological text. That means it tells you how things happened in order. If you were going to describe your school day in chronological order, what would be the first thing you describe? (Possible response: arriving at school, walking into the classroom, first lesson of the day) Tell students to look for clue words, such as first, next, and then, to understand the sequence in the text.

Display a T-chart graphic organizer. At the top of the left column, write “Chronological Structure.” Work with the class to fill in the column with what they know about chronological structure and how it helps them understand the writer’s ideas. For example: “explains what happened in order”

Text Features Say: This text has different parts with headings. The headings are in large, bold print. What is the first heading you see? (A First Plan for the Government) Read the text on screen 3 (Printable Text p. x). Ask: What was the first plan for the government? (The Articles of Confederation)

Return to the T-chart. At the top of the right column, write “Headings.” Work with the class to fill in the column with examples of how headings help readers understand the text. For example: “tell the topic or main idea of a section”

After Reading

Vocabulary

After students have read How the Constitution Works, have them add any words to their word web. Display these images from How the Constitution Works to help students as they think about vocabulary words, such as constitution, bill, and executive.

[images from the book for each word for the teacher to project: TK]

Discuss Across Texts

Encourage students to make connections between How the Constitution Works and other texts they have read on related subjects.

  • What other facts have you read about the beginning of the United States?
  • What else do you know about the different parts or branches of the US government? How did this text add to your understanding?
  • Think back to Why Do We Have a Government? Why do people need governments? Why do you think a government needs a constitution?

Track the Texts: After the discussion, have students return to the The Role of Government: Track the Texts graphic organizer. Have them fill in the How the Constitution Works row with something they learned about how government works.

DISCUSS THE BIG QUESTION

Lead a class discussion around this text’s Big Question: What role does the government play in improving our lives? As students share their ideas, draw out more information from them with follow-up questions such as, “Can you say more about that?” or “What are some examples of that?”

  • What did the founders think that the role of the government should be? Why did some of them disagree about the role of the government?
  • Why do you think the people who wrote the Constitution wanted to give rights to the people? How did the Constitution protect people’s rights?

Write About the Big Question

Have students imagine they are a delegate at the Constitutional Convention. Have them write a short letter to someone who is not at the convention, such as a family member or friend at home, explaining what they are doing and why it is important.

Expanding Word Webs Have students review the word webs they have created for the texts they have read. Put students in pairs or small groups and have them make a larger word web on a large sheet of paper. Have them write government in the center and choose words to add around the web. Encourage students to explain why they added the words they did.

Discuss the Text Set's Big Question

Guide a class discussion around the Big Question: What role does the government play in improving our lives? Encourage students to bring in knowledge they have learned from all the texts they have read in the text set, using as many of the words from their word webs as they can.

  • Which text was most helpful in answering the Big Question? What did you learn from the text?
  • How was the US government designed to work? Was this a good way to organize the government? Why or why not?
  • What did you learn about how the government can help people? What else would you like to learn about the government?
  • What responsibilities do different parts of the government have? How do they play different roles in people’s lives?

Culminating Project

Have students apply what they’ve learned from the text set to demonstrate and enhance their understanding. Review the Project Rubric with students, explaining in detail the expectations for the project.

PROJECT: Letter to a Government Official

Help students brainstorm important issues that affect your community and how the government can help. Then, choose a government official who represents your area. Students can choose an issue that you brainstormed and write a letter to the official about it. Review formal letter writing format before students begin writing. Remind them to review what they wrote in Write About the Big Question for each text to help them think about what they have learned about government. When students have finished writing, gather the letters and send them to the official.

Some students may benefit from working with a partner. Students may use the Project Template to help them organize and clarify their thinking as they plan their letters. Encourage students to use the vocabulary they’ve learned from the texts.

Prepare to Share

Provide time for students to practice sharing their culminating project. If they are working with a partner, remind them to work collaboratively and follow agreed upon rules, norms, and protocols.

Use the bulleted points below to model good speaking and listening skills.

  • When I share my project, I look at the audience. I don't keep my eyes down.
  • As I speak, I make sure that everyone can hear what I am saying.
  • I pronounce each word clearly and correctly.
  • I do not rush, but speak clearly and at a natural pace.
  • I pay attention with my eyes and ears when others are speaking.

Celebrate!

Have students share their projects with the class. As students share, be sure to model and encourage strong listening skills.

Discussion Tips

An important part of building a vibrant classroom culture is creating an atmosphere in which all students feel empowered to participate in powerful classroom conversation. You may have noticed that your students who are naturally extroverted often participate freely in a whole-class discussion, while those who are more introverted or, perhaps, who come from cultures that respect different classroom norms, may need encouragement to share. You can use sentence prompts like these to invite all students into the conversation:

“That’s so interesting … please tell me more!”

“Can someone explain that in a different way?”

“What are some examples of that?”

“Can someone add on to that idea?”

Sharing and discussing ideas will often involve agreeing and/or disagreeing with what others say. As students listen carefully and respond thoughtfully to their classmates’ ideas, encourage them to express themselves in respectful and constructive ways. You may wish to post these sentence starters prominently, so students can refer to them often:

“I agree, and I also think fill in the blank line__________ .”

“I mostly agree, but fill in the blank line__________ .”

“I like what you just said because fill in the blank line__________ .”

“I respectfully disagree because fill in the blank line__________ .”
